The fortune cookie is Japanese签语饼其实是日本的

A 19th-century Kyoto temple. A 1942 San Francisco internment. How a Japanese rice cracker became the souvenir of American Chinese food.十九世纪京都的一间神社。1942 年旧金山的一次拘留。一片日本米饼如何变成了美式中餐的纪念品。

A fortune cookie has never been served in China. Walk into a Beijing or Shanghai or Hong Kong restaurant and ask for one and the waiter will smile politely and have no idea what you mean. This is, depending on your nationality, either surprising or completely obvious. It is consistently surprising to most Americans, who have eaten fortune cookies after every Chinese-American restaurant meal of their lives. This essay is the genealogy.签语饼，从来没在中国餐厅出现过。走进北京、上海或香港的任何一家中餐馆要一个，服务员会礼貌地笑，不知道你在说什么。这件事——视乎你的国籍——要么令人惊讶，要么显而易见。对大多数美国人来说，是持续地惊讶的——他们这辈子在美式中餐馆吃完饭都收到一个。本篇是这道东西的族谱。

Chapter 1 · The Kyoto cracker第一章 · 京都的米饼

In the late 19th century, bakeries near Fushimi Inari shrine in Kyoto sold a small cracker called tsujiura senbei (辻占煎餅) — "fortune cracker." It was made of rice flour, sesame, miso, and sugar, baked in a hinged iron mold and folded around a slip of paper bearing an omen or a poem. It was larger than the modern American fortune cookie, darker in color, and faintly miso-savory rather than purely sweet. Photographic evidence dates the cookie to at least 1878. A wood-block-print illustration from an 1880 Japanese book shows a baker pulling them, freshly folded, from a hot mold.19 世纪末，京都伏见稻荷大社附近的店铺卖一种小米饼，叫"辻占煎饼"——"占卜煎饼"。它用米粉、芝麻、味噌、糖做成，在合页铁模里烤，对折时夹一小条写着吉凶或诗的纸。它比现代美版签语饼大、颜色更深，带味噌的咸鲜，而不是纯甜。有照片可证至少存在到 1878 年。1880 年一本日本书里的木刻插图，画着师傅从热模里夹出刚对折好的饼。

Chapter 2 · San Francisco, 1907–1942第二章 · 旧金山，1907–1942

Japanese immigrants brought the cracker to California in the late 1800s. Two competing accounts identify the introducer to the United States: Makoto Hagiwara, the gardener of Golden Gate Park's Japanese Tea Garden, who is said to have served the cookies to guests starting around 1907; and Suyeichi Okamura, who manufactured them at his Benkyodo confectionery in Japantown San Francisco from 1906. Both are credible. By the 1920s, fortune cookies were a known San Francisco souvenir, baked by Japanese-American confectioners. The fortune slips were initially in Japanese; bilingual versions followed by 1925.19 世纪末，日本移民把这种米饼带到了加州。关于"谁先在美国推广"，有两位竞争者：旧金山金门公园日本茶园园主萩原诚——传说他从 1907 年起向访客提供这种饼；以及冈村惠一，从 1906 年起在旧金山日本町的勉强堂菓子店生产签语饼。两位都可信。1920 年代，签语饼已是旧金山的纪念品，由日裔点心师傅烘焙。最初签条是日语，1925 年前后出现日英双语版本。

Chapter 3 · The internment, 1942第三章 · 拘留，1942

In February 1942 the United States interned approximately 120,000 Japanese-Americans. The Benkyodo confectionery in San Francisco closed. The Hagiwara family was sent to Topaz, Utah. Hundreds of small Japanese-American bakeries shuttered overnight, their hinged iron molds left behind in shuttered storefronts. Chinese-American bakeries — operating across the Pacific from the same family of pastry traditions but legally permitted to remain open — moved in. They acquired the molds. They acquired the recipes, modified slightly toward American taste (sweeter, lighter in color, paper slip in English). By 1949, fortune cookies were so widely associated with Chinese-American restaurants that the original Japanese provenance had been substantially forgotten.1942 年 2 月，美国拘留了约 12 万日裔。旧金山的勉强堂关闭。萩原家被送到犹他州托帕兹。几百家小型日裔烘焙店一夜歇业，铁模留在了被封的店里。华裔烘焙店——同样源自东亚点心传统，但法律上可以继续营业——接手了。他们接过了铁模、配方，按美式口味略调（更甜、颜色更浅、签条换成英文）。1949 年前，签语饼已经被广泛归入美式中餐范畴，原始的日本出身基本被遗忘。

Chapter 4 · The lawsuit, 1983第四章 · 法庭，1983

In 1983 the San Francisco Court of Historical Review held a mock trial titled "Who Invented the Fortune Cookie?" — pitting San Francisco's claim (via Hagiwara) against Los Angeles's claim (via the Hong Kong Noodle Company, a Chinese-American bakery that had begun making them by 1918). The mock court ruled for San Francisco. The verdict was non-binding but historically useful: it formalized the consensus that fortune cookies were American, with Japanese roots, popularized through Chinese-American restaurants.1983 年，旧金山"历史评议法庭"举行了一场题为"签语饼是谁发明的？"的模拟审判——旧金山方（萩原家）对洛杉矶方（香港面条公司，一家 1918 年前已经在做这种饼的华裔烘焙店）。模拟法庭判旧金山胜。判决没有法律效力但有历史价值：它把"签语饼是美国的、有日本根、由美式中餐推广开"这一共识写进了纪录。

Chapter 5 · Coda — China discovers the fortune cookie第五章 · 尾声 · 中国"反向引进"签语饼

In 1989, the American firm Wonton Food attempted to introduce fortune cookies to China. The product was sold in test markets in Hong Kong, then Beijing. The campaign failed within six months. Chinese consumers found the cracker bland and the concept — a slip of paper inside a baked dessert — culturally incoherent. The firm withdrew. Wonton Food's then-CEO famously told the press, "the cookies were too American for China." A more enduring punchline: a food invented by Japanese-Americans, marketed by Chinese-Americans, was rejected by China as too American.1989 年，美国温顿食品公司尝试把签语饼引进中国。先在香港试点，后转北京。半年内失败。中国消费者觉得饼太淡、概念——一片烤甜点里夹张纸——文化上读不通。公司撤出。当时的 CEO 对媒体说出了名言："这饼对中国来说太美国了。"更经久的笑话是：一件由日裔美国人发明、由华裔美国人推广的东西，最后被中国以"太美国"为由拒绝了。

What to take from this从这件事可以读到什么

The fortune cookie is the perfect parable of diaspora cuisine. Its history is not a corruption story (the "fake-Chinese cookie") nor a triumph story (the "Asian-American invention that conquered America"). It is a story of specific people in specific cities, of an unjust internment that altered who owned a recipe, of decades of restaurant labor by two immigrant communities that the American imagination has compressed into one. The cookie is American. It was Japanese. It is, today, what Chinese-Americans bring to your table as a wink. That is a good and serious thing to be.签语饼是流散菜系的一个完美寓言。它的历史不是"假中餐"的堕落故事，也不是"亚裔美国人征服美国"的凯歌。它是关于具体的人、具体的城市，关于一次不公正的拘留改变了配方归谁所有，关于两代移民几十年的辛苦——而美国人的想象把这两个社群压成了一个。这饼是美国的。它曾是日本的。今天，是华裔美国人在饭后递到你面前的一个小礼物。这是个值得认真对待的身份。

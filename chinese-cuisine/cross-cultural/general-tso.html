The strange life of General Tso左宗棠鸡奇异的一生

A 19th-century Hunan general. A 1970s Taipei chef. A late-Cold War New York. The journey of a dish that almost no one in China has ever eaten.一个晚清湘军名将。一个 1970 年代的台北厨子。一个冷战末的纽约。一道在中国本土几乎没人吃过的菜，如何漂洋过海。

In the early 1990s, a documentarian named Ian Cheney travelled to Hunan province in search of the man behind one of America's most ordered Chinese dishes. He carried a photograph: a glossy plate of orange, sweet, deeply fried boneless chicken in a tangle of sauce, garnished with broccoli. He showed it to chefs in Changsha. They had never seen it. He showed it to descendants of the actual General Zuo Zongtang, the 19th-century military commander whose name the dish bears. They were polite, but they had also never seen it.1990 年代初，纪录片导演 Ian Cheney 去湖南找一道菜背后的那个人——这道菜在美国是中餐外卖里点得最多的之一。他带了张照片：油亮的橙色、甜口、去骨深炸鸡块裹一团酱汁，配西兰花点缀。他拿去问长沙的厨子。他们没见过。他拿去问真正的左宗棠（晚清军事统帅）的后人。他们很客气，但也没见过。

This is the story of General Tso's chicken — a dish named for a man who never ate it, born in a kitchen its inventor would not recognize, and beloved by a country its inventor never quite called home.这就是左宗棠鸡——一道以一个从未吃过它的人命名的菜，诞生于一间它的发明者后来认不出的厨房，最终被发明者从未真正称为家乡的国家所深爱。

Chapter 1 · The general (1812–1885)第一章 · 那位将军（1812–1885）

Zuo Zongtang (左宗棠) was, by any measure, one of the most consequential men of the late Qing dynasty. A military commander from a Hunan landowning family, he suppressed the Taiping Rebellion, recovered Xinjiang for the Qing in a brutal multi-year campaign, and was, for decades, viceroy of two consecutive provinces. The dish — to the extent it relates to him at all — relates to Hunan, his birthplace; the chili-and-vinegar profile of the modern dish gestures toward the actual Hunanese kitchen, which is famously hot, sour, and far less sweet than the American version. But Zuo himself was a known traditionalist and almost certainly never ate anything close to the dish that bears his name.左宗棠（1812–1885）按任何标准都是晚清最有分量的人物之一。湖南地主家庭出身的军事统帅，平太平、用多年硬仗收复新疆、连任两省总督。这道菜——若与他真有关联——也只是关联到他的出生地湖南：菜里的辣与醋的轮廓，确实指向湖南本菜系的方向。但湖南菜以辣、酸、不甜出名，与美式的甜橙色版本相去甚远。左本人是出名的守旧派，几乎可以断定从未吃过一道叫他名字的菜。

Chapter 2 · The chef in Taipei (1952–1973)第二章 · 台北的那位厨子（1952–1973）

The actual inventor is Peng Chang-kuei (彭长贵), a Hunanese chef who fled the mainland in 1949 with the Nationalist government and settled in Taipei. Peng cooked for the Republic of China military and for high government banquets. In 1952 or 1955 — accounts differ — Peng improvised a dish at a state banquet honoring Admiral Arthur W. Radford, then Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of the United States. The dish was a Hunanese-style fried chicken in a savory-sour-spicy sauce, dark and complex. Peng later said he named it after Zuo Zongtang as a tribute to a fellow Hunanese hero.真正的发明人是彭长贵——湖南厨子，1949 年随国民政府撤台，在台北落脚。彭为军方与高层宴席掌勺。1952 或 1955 年（各说不一），他在为时任美国参谋长联席会议主席 Arthur W. Radford 上将访台的国宴上即兴做了一道菜——湖南式炸鸡，配咸酸辣兼具的暗色复合酱。彭后来说，他以左宗棠之名命名，是向同乡英雄致敬。

Crucially: the original was not sweet. It was not orange. It was not battered into a crisp shell. It was not served with broccoli. Peng's General Tso was a serious Hunanese composition with a clean, almost austere profile — vinegar bright, chili-warm, soy-deep.关键的是：原版不甜。不是橙色。不裹厚壳。不配西兰花。彭长贵那版的左宗棠鸡是认真的湘菜结构——醋亮、辣温、酱厚，干净，甚至偏素。

Chapter 3 · The migration to New York (1973–1977)第三章 · 移到纽约（1973–1977）

In 1973, Peng opened a restaurant in midtown Manhattan. He brought his General Tso's chicken with him. American diners — used to the sweet, deep-fried, easily-shareable Cantonese-American dishes of the previous half-century — were polite about the original. They liked the chicken, but the dish in its Hunanese form was harder to read. Within four years, two competing Manhattan chefs — Tsung Ting Wang (T.T. Wang) at Shun Lee West and Yi Ben "David" Keh at Hunam — had each developed sweetened, brighter, more clearly orange versions. By the late 1970s, the sweet version had won. Peng, in interviews near the end of his life, was openly bemused. The dish he named after the Hunanese hero had become an American invention with a Hunanese accent.1973 年，彭在曼哈顿中城开了餐厅，把左宗棠鸡带了过去。美国食客——之前半个世纪习惯了"甜、深炸、好分"的粤式美餐——对原版礼貌地接受。他们喜欢鸡，但湘菜式的那一版较难解读。四年内，曼哈顿另外两位华裔厨子——顺利酒家西馆的王宗鼎（T.T. Wang）与湖南酒家的柯一蓓（David Keh）——各自做出了甜口、亮色、更橙的版本。1970 年代末，甜版本胜出。彭长贵晚年的访谈里，对这件事公开摇头：他用乡贤命名的菜，已经变成了一道带湖南口音的美国菜。

Chapter 4 · What the dish became第四章 · 菜变成了什么

By 1985, General Tso's was on the menu of an estimated 60% of Chinese-American restaurants in the United States. The "canonical" American version: chunks of dark-meat chicken battered in egg-and-cornstarch, deep-fried, tossed with a sauce of soy, sugar, vinegar, ginger, garlic, and dried chili — sometimes with a splash of orange juice or hoisin for the now-defining orange tint. The dish absorbed the role that sweet-and-sour pork had played for an older generation: the sweet, savory, fork-friendly anchor of a takeout order. Steamed broccoli on the side was a 1980s health-conscious addition.1985 年，左宗棠鸡进入了美国估计 60% 中餐馆的菜单。"经典"美式版本：鸡腿肉切块裹蛋淀粉浆深炸，与酱油、糖、醋、姜、蒜、干辣椒调成的酱汁同炒——通常再加一点橙汁或海鲜酱，让它呈那种"标志性"的橙色。这道菜接过了甜酸肉在上一代人那里的角色：外卖菜单上那道甜咸、好用叉子、好分的主菜。一侧蒸西兰花是 1980 年代健康意识的加料。

Chapter 5 · A note on authenticity第五章 · 关于"正宗"

It is fashionable to mock General Tso's for being "inauthentic." We do not. The dish has a real, traceable lineage — Hunanese in inspiration, Taipei in conception, New York in execution — and it has fed three generations of Americans, in many cases their first encounter with anything Chinese. To call it inauthentic is to misunderstand what authenticity is. It is a real dish, with a real history, owed to specific named people. That is all authenticity ever was.嘲笑左宗棠鸡"不正宗"是个流行做法。我们不参与。这道菜有真实的、可考的谱系——湖南来灵感，台北做出来，纽约定型——它喂饱了三代美国人，其中很多人是通过这道菜第一次接触任何中国味道。说它"不正宗"，是没明白"正宗"是什么。它是真菜，有真历史，要感谢具体的、有名有姓的人。"正宗"从来就只是这个意思。
